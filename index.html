The Adoption of Legalized Sports Betting across the United States.

Up until several years ago, the only place in the United States where you could legally place a wager on a professional or college sporting event was in the state of Nevada.  A landmark Supreme Court decision in 2018 changed that.  As we approach the year 2021, betting on sporting events is now legal in over 20 states with more states currently on the path toward legalization.

Historical Timeline

March 19, 1931

Nevada legalizes various forms of gambling and becomes the only state with legalized gambling.  However, betting on sports remained unregulated by the state and, as a result, sports betting largely remained an illegal, undeground activity.

April, 1949

Nevada sets regulatory framework for legalized sports betting and opens the first government-sanctioned sportsbook.  In addition, bookmakers across the state now had the permission and legal authority to accept bets on all professional sports as well as horse racing.

March, 1951

The Federal Government imposes a 10% excise tax on all Nevada sportsbooks.  This ultimately led to the early demise of the legal sportsbook industry as the newly imposed tax made it impossible for them to compete with underground sports betting operations, most of which were run by organized crime.

September 13, 1961

As a deterrent to illegal sports betting rings run by the mob throughout the country, The Federal Wire Act was signed into law by President John F. Kennedy.  It is a federal law that prohibits the transfer of information relating to sports betting and the placing of wagers on any sporting event via the use of wire communications.  It also prohibits the receipt of money as a result of bets or wagers via these same channels.

September 13, 1961

An extension of The Federal Wire Act, The Travel Act was signed into law.  This act makes it a crime to use the U.S. Mail service to engage in criminal activity. The act explicility targeted sports betting.

September 13, 1961

Also an extension of The Federal Wire Act, The Interstate Transportation Wagering Paraphernalia Act was also signed into law.  This law made it illegal for anyone to cross state lines with materials relating to, or engaging in, sports betting.

August, 1974

The United States Congress lowers the tax rate on sports betting operations from 10% to 2%.  With the lower tax rate, legal sports betting was once again an economically viable business for bookmakers in Nevada and the industry took off.

May, 1975

Following the lowering of the tax rate, the first sportsbook located within a casino opens at the Stardust casino in Las Vegas.  Soon after, nearly every casino in Las Vegas had a sportsbook.

March, 1976

Delaware approves a form of sports betting, parlay betting, when it introduces NFL parlay cards to its state lottery offerings.  

August, 1984

The Federal Government reduces the tax rate on sports betting from 2% to .5% making sportsbooks even more economically viable.  Sportsbook growth explodes across Nevada.

November, 1986

Montana voters approve the creation of the Montana Lottery with limited sports pools and fantasy sports betting options.

November, 1989

Oregon approves a form of sports betting with the creation of the Oregon Sports Action parlay game offering NFL and NBA parlay cards.

October 28, 1992

The Professional and Amateur Sports Protection Act (PASPA) was signed into law by President George H. W. Bush.  PASPA effectively banned sports betting in the United States, with exemptions for sports betting products already offered in Nevada, Oregon, Delaware, and Montana.

January 17, 1996

The first wager on a sporting event was made online through an offshore online sportsbook.  Soon after, the offshore betting industry, which largely targeted United States consumers, experienced explosive growth as these online sportsbooks had no physical presence on U.S. soil and were free from any legal repercussions.  In the following years, Americans will bet over $1 billion on sporting events at offshore online sportsbooks.

October 3, 2006

The Unlawful Internet Gambling Enforcement Act of 2006 (UIGEA) was signed into law by President George W. Bush.  In practice, it simply made the movement of money into and out of gaming sites illegal, thus rendering it impossible to legally bet online within the United States.  The law explicility targeted offshore online betting operations that served US consumers.

May, 2009

Delaware uses the PASPA exception it was granted to fully legalize all forms of sports betting in the state.  However, the courts limit Delaware's sports betting offerings to only the parlay bets it approved in 1976.

November, 2011

New Jersey, via a voter referendum, legalizes sports betting.  The new law will be the beginning of a multi-year legal battle that ultimately brings about the repeal of PASPA.

January, 2012

Professional sports leagues and the NCAA sue New Jersey to prevent their newly legalized sports betting law from being enacted.

February, 2013

New Jersey loses it's case in US District Court as the court sides with the NCAA and professional sports leagues.  Sports betting remains illegal in New Jersey.  New Jersey appeals decision.

September, 2013

New Jersey loses it's appeal as the Third Cicuit Court upholds the prior ruling.  New Jersey will appeal the case, but the Supreme Court will eventually decline to hear the case.

November, 2013

New York voters approve a referendum allowing commerical casinos in the state.  The referendum also preauthorizes legal sports betting at these casinos in case federal law is ever changed to allow sports betting.

September, 2014

New Jersey tries again and creates new law that conforms to the court's prior arguments.  Rather than trying to legalize and regulate sports betting, this new law only decriminalizes sports betting in the state.

October, 2014

Professional sports leagues and the NCAA sue New Jersey once again over its new law.

November, 2014

The District Court again sides with the NCAA and professional sports leagues and strikes down the new law.  New Jersey again appeals the decision.

November, 2015

The Third Circuit Court of Appeals also sides with the NCAA and professional sports leagues.

November, 2016

New Jersey petitions the Supreme Court to hear its case.

March, 2017

Mississippi preauthorizes legalized sports betting with the hope that the state can offer sports betting if New Jersey is successful in its case before the Supreme Court.

June, 2017

The Supreme Court decides to hear New Jersey's case.

October, 2017

Pennsylvania's legislature preauthorizes legalized sports betting.

December, 2017

The Supreme Court hears oral arguments in New Jersey's case.  Many believe New Jersey is favored to win its case.

March, 2018

With New Jersey's chances looking good in its case at The Supreme Court, West Virginia passes a bill which legalizes sports betting in the state.

May, 2018

New Jersey wins case as SCOTUS rules PASPA Unconstitutional paving the way for states to take advantage of the 10th Amendment and legalize and regulate sports betting as they see fit.

June, 2018

Delaware, with sports betting laws on the books since 2009, quickly expands its sports betting options.

June, 2018
